2. Central tendency of the dataset

Mean

Let's say you have the following dataset of ages: 25, 30, 22, 28, 35.

Sum of all values: 25 + 30 + 22 + 28 + 35 = 140 Number of values: 5 Mean: 140 / 5 = 28 Therefore, the mean age in this dataset is 28.

Important Notes:

The mean is sensitive to outliers (extreme values). A single very large or very small value can significantly affect the mean.

The mean is typically used for datasets with a roughly symmetrical distribution. For skewed data, the median might be a more representative measure of central tendency.

Out[131]:
38.58164675532078

Median

The median is the middle value in a dataset that has been sorted in ascending order. It separates the lower half of the data from the upper half.

Here's how to calculate the median:

Sort the data: Arrange the values in ascending order (from smallest to largest). Find the middle: If the dataset has an odd number of values: The median is the middle value. If the dataset has an even number of values: The median is the average of the two middle values. Examples:

Odd number of values:

Dataset: 22, 25, 28, 30, 35

Sorted data: 22, 25, 28, 30, 35 Median: 28 Even number of values:

Dataset: 22, 25, 28, 30

Sorted data: 22, 25, 28, 30 Median: (25 + 28) / 2 = 26.5 Important Notes:

The median is less affected by outliers than the mean. Extreme values don't influence the middle value as much. The median is often used for datasets with skewed distributions, where the mean might be misleading. In Pandas, you can calculate the median of a DataFrame or Series using the df.median() method. It handles NaN (Not a Number) values by ignoring them.

37.0

Mode

The mode is the value that appears most frequently in a dataset. A dataset can have one mode (unimodal), more than one mode (multimodal), or no mode at all if all values occur with the same frequency.

Here's how to calculate the mode:

Count occurrences: Determine how many times each value appears in the dataset. Identify the most frequent: The value(s) with the highest count is/are the mode(s).

Examples:

a. Unimodal:

Dataset: 22, 25, 28, 30, 35, 25

  1. 22 appears once.
  2. 25 appears twice.
  3. 28 appears once.
  4. 30 appears once.
  5. 35 appears once.

Mode: 25

b. Multimodal:

Dataset: 22, 25, 28, 30, 25, 28

  1. 22 appears once.
  2. 25 appears twice.
  3. 28 appears twice.
  4. 30 appears once.

Modes: 25 and 28

c. No mode:

Dataset: 22, 25, 28, 30

All values appear once.

Mode: None

Important Notes:

The mode is the only measure of central tendency that can be used for categorical data (e.g., colors, types of fruit). A dataset can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes. In Pandas, you can calculate the mode of a DataFrame or Series using the df.mode() method.

Variance

Example:

Let's say your dataset is: [2, 4, 6, 8]

Calculate the Mean: (2 + 4 + 6 + 8) / 4 = 5

Calculate the Squared Differences:

(2 - 5) = 9

(4 - 5) = 1

(6 - 5) = 1

(8 - 5) = 9

Calculate the Variance:

(9 + 1 + 1 + 9) / 4 = 5

So, the variance of the dataset [2, 4, 6, 8] is 5.

Key Points:

Variance measures how much the data points deviate from the mean. A higher variance indicates that the data is more spread out. A lower variance indicates that the data is more clustered around the mean. Variance is always a non-negative value. The square root of the variance gives you the standard deviation, which is another common measure of dispersion.

Standard Deviation vs Variance

When to use what:

Standard Deviation:

  1. When you need to understand the spread of data in the context of the original units.
  2. When communicating variability to a non-technical audience.
  3. When comparing the variability of different datasets with the same units.

Variance:

  1. When performing statistical calculations or modeling, as it has convenient mathematical properties.
  2. When comparing the variability of different datasets with different units (since the units are squared, they become irrelevant for comparison).

In essence:

Standard deviation is more interpretable. Variance is more mathematically useful.

Example:

If you're analyzing the heights of students, standard deviation (e.g., "The standard deviation is 5 cm") would be easier to understand than variance (e.g., "The variance is 25 cm"). However, variance might be preferred in certain statistical formulas.

Ultimately, the choice depends on the specific context and purpose of your analysis.

Quartile Analysis

Dataset: 6, 7, 15, 36, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43, 47, 49

Sorted: 6, 7, 15, 36, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43, 47, 49

Median (Q2): 40

Q1: Median of {6, 7, 15, 36, 39} = 15

Q3: Median of {41, 42, 43, 47, 49} = 43

Key Points:

Quartiles help understand the spread and distribution of data. The Interquartile Range (IQR) is Q3 - Q1 and represents the spread of the middle 50% of the data. Quartiles are used for box plots and identifying potential outliers.

Q1, Q2, Q3, and IQR are all measures of position that divide a dataset into groups, providing insights into the distribution and spread of the data. Here's the significance of each:

Q1 (First Quartile):

  1. Marks the 25th percentile of the data.
  2. 25% of the values in the dataset fall below Q1.
  3. Helps identify the lower end of the data's spread.

Q2 (Second Quartile/Median):

  1. Marks the 50th percentile of the data.
  2. Divides the data in half, with 50% of values falling below and 50% above.
  3. Represents the middle value of the dataset.

Q3 (Third Quartile):

  1. Marks the 75th percentile of the data.
  2. 75% of the values in the dataset fall below Q3.
  3. Helps identify the upper end of the data's spread.

IQR (Interquartile Range):

  1. Calculated as Q3 - Q1.
  2. Represents the range of the middle 50% of the data.
  3. Less affected by extreme values (outliers) than the overall range.

Used to:

  1. Identify potential outliers.
  2. Understand the variability of the data.
  3. Construct box plots for visualizing the distribution.

Overall Significance:

  1. Provide a more detailed picture of the data's distribution than just the mean or median.
  2. Help identify where the majority of the data lies.
  3. Useful for comparing distributions across different datasets.
  4. Essential for understanding the spread and variability of data.

4. Understanding the Data distribution

Skewness

Skewness is a measure of the asymmetry of the distribution of a dataset. Here's how it's calculated:

  1. Calculate the Mean ():

Find the average of all the values in the dataset.

  1. Calculate the Standard Deviation ():

A measure of how spread out the data is around the mean (see previous explanations for how to calculate this).

  1. Calculate the Skewness:

This involves calculating the third standardized moment. Here's the formula:

Skewness = (xi - ) / (N * )

Where:

xi = each value in the dataset

= the mean of the dataset

= the standard deviation of the dataset

N = the number of values in the dataset

Interpretation:

Skewness = 0: The distribution is perfectly symmetrical. Skewness > 0 (Positive Skew): The distribution has a longer tail on the right side. The mean is greater than the median. Skewness < 0 (Negative Skew): The distribution has a longer tail on the left side. The mean is less than the median.

In simpler terms:

Imagine a bell curve. If the bell curve is stretched towards the right, it's positively skewed. If it's stretched towards the left, it's negatively skewed.

Why is skewness important?

It helps understand the shape of the data distribution.

Many statistical models assume a normal distribution (symmetrical). Skewness helps assess if this assumption is valid.

It can influence the choice of appropriate summary statistics (mean vs. median).

Kurtosis is a measure of the "tailedness" and peakedness of the distribution of a dataset. Here's how it's calculated:

  1. Calculate the Mean ():

Find the average of all the values in the dataset.

  1. Calculate the Standard Deviation ():

Interpretation:

Kurtosis = 3 (or 0 in some normalizations): The distribution is mesokurtic, similar to a normal distribution.

Kurtosis > 3 (Leptokurtic): The distribution has heavier tails and a sharper peak than a normal distribution. The data is more concentrated around the mean, with more outliers.

Kurtosis < 3 (Platykurtic): The distribution has lighter tails and a flatter peak than a normal distribution. The data is less concentrated around the mean.

In simpler terms:

Imagine a bell curve.

A leptokurtic distribution is like a tall, skinny bell with long tails.

A platykurtic distribution is like a wide, flat bell with short tails.

Why is kurtosis important?

It helps understand the shape of the data distribution, particularly how extreme values are distributed. It can indicate the presence of outliers. It's used in various statistical tests and models.

Summary : Skewness Vs Kurtosis

Both skewness and kurtosis describe the shape of a distribution, but they focus on different aspects:

Skewness

Measures the asymmetry of a distribution.

Example: Imagine the distribution of income. It's likely to be positively skewed, with a long tail on the right side due to a few very high earners.

When to use it:

To understand if the data is concentrated on one side of the distribution. To choose appropriate summary statistics (mean vs. median). The mean is more influenced by extreme values in a skewed distribution.

Kurtosis

Measures the tailedness and peakedness of a distribution.

Example: Consider the distribution of exam scores. A platykurtic distribution might indicate that students' scores are spread out relatively evenly, while a leptokurtic distribution might suggest that most students scored close to the average with a few outliers at the extremes.

When to use it:

To understand the presence and concentration of extreme values (outliers). To assess if the data follows a normal distribution, which is mesokurtic.

Choosing between skewness and kurtosis:

  1. The choice depends on what you want to investigate about the data's shape:

  2. If you're interested in the balance of the distribution and the presence of extreme values on one side, focus on skewness.

  3. If you're interested in the concentration of data around the mean and the presence of outliers, focus on kurtosis.

Often, it's helpful to consider both skewness and kurtosis together to get a comprehensive understanding of the distribution's shape.
